1.0 Word Class in Latin

There are 8 classes of word in Latin: “noun, pronoun, verb, adverb, adjective, conjunction, interjection, and preposition.” There’s NO ARTICLE (such as, “the” or “a/an” in English) in Latin.

  • Noun, such as Caesar(Emperor), Domus(house), and Canis(dog).
  • Pronoun, such as:
    • Meus(Mine): Tabula est mea. (The tablet is mine)
    • Ille(That): Ille vir. (That man)
    • Qui(Who): Qui fecit hoc? (Who did this?)
  • Adjective, such as:
    • Altus(Tall): Altus homo. (Tall man)
    • Albus(White): Alba domus. (White house | PS Not the one in the U.S.)
  • Verb, such as Manducare(to eat), Dormire(to sleep), and Loqui(to talk).
  • Adverb, such as:
    • Breviter(briefly): Breviter loqui. (to talk breifly)
    • Multam(alot): Multam manducare. (to eat a lot)
  • Conjunction, such as et(and), sed(but), and nisi(unless).
  • Interjection, such as heu(phew).
  • Preposition, such as:
    • In(in, at): In schola sumus. (We are at school)
    • Supra(on, over): Domus supra montem. (A house on the mountain)
    • Ad(to): Ad scholam eo. (I go to school)

1.1 Genders

There are 3 genders for nouns in Latin: masculinum, femininum, and neutrum. There’s no regular rules for us to tell if a noun is masculine, feminine, or neutral. However, after we have gotten more and more familiar with this language, we will still find out some rules for some sets of nouns. For example: nouns end with -tas is always feminine, like caritas(charity/love) and majestas(majesty).

1.2 Personal Pronouns

You can see all the personal pronouns here in Latin in the following table.

Personal pronoun Singular Plural
First person Ego(I) Nos(we)
Second person Tu(you) Vos(you)
Third person ille (he) / illa (she) /illud (it) Illi / Illae / Illa

You may notice that, the third-person part contains 3 different pronouns, and when they come to be plural, we use Illi(they) to refer to things that contain 1. only masculine nouns, or 2. a set of nouns with more than one gender (e.g. Leo (lion/m.) et praedam (prey/f.) = Illi) . We use Illae when the nouns that we refer to are all feminine, and the same rule also fits illa.

1.2.1 Active Voice & Passive Voice

  • Active voice: Ego manduco carnem. (I eat meat)
  • Passive voice: Caro manducata est a me. (The meat was eaten by me)

“Caro” in the second sentence means “meat” in Nominative (when the noun is a subject), while “carnem” also means “meat,” but under “Accusative” , which means that this noun serves as a direct object. So you will also see how different cases influence the conjugation of all the words in Latin. AND PLEASE DO NOTICE THAT, conjugation of nouns and verb is the most important part, and the most challenging part for you to learn, so please make sure to pay extra attention when you encounter a new word, and try to memorize it by heart!
