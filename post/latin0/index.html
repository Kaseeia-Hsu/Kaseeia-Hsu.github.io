0.1 Purposes of Learning Latin Language

Although it’s an undeniable truth that Latin language itself has now become an heritage of time, men will still say the influence and the importance of Latin are inmeasureable. Latin served not only as the official language of Roman Empire, but also the official language of most of the nations in the Medieval era. Along with the language itself, many great works written in Latin also left for us to read, and to research; for example, Seneca’s Naturales Questions, Iulius Caeser’s Bellum Gallicum, or even the Bible.

So, the main purpose of learning Latin must be “becoming able to read ancient works.” However, as time went by, the language itself has changed a lot too, for example, in Cicero(106~43B.C.) and Plautus(254~184B.C.)’s era, the word “Imperator” means “commander,” but after Thurinus(or the so-called “Augustus”) seized the throne, the word started to refer to “emporer.”

0.2 Knowing How Basic Latin Works

In this part, we will see a few Latin sentences, and the purpose of showing them is not to intimidate you from Latin–instead of that, what you need to do is: try your best to find similarities between Latin and languages that you’re familiar with, such as English, Chinese, or German……etc. And you will see a lot of concepts or parts which you’re definitely familiar with, such as “subject,” “verb,” “direct/indirect object,” and maybe, there will also be something like “prepositional complement,” “adjective,” and “adverb” in your sentence.

  1. Leo errans spinam calcavit. (Phaedrus, app., 15)

= A lion walking (subject+adj.) a thorn (object) trampled upon (verb).

Which means “a walking lion trampled upon a thorn.” So you may have found out that the word order in a sentence in Latin is: S+O+V, while in English, it is S+V+O. However, the order of SVO works in Latin, too, but this order itself is not widely used especially in formal writting.

  1. Duo homines, unus fallax et alius verax, simul iter agebant. (Phaedrus, app., 24)

= Two men (subject), one deceitful and the other truthful, simultaneously (adv.) the way (object) traveling (verb).

Which means “Two men, one deceitful and the other truthful, simultaneously traveling on the way.”

  1. Nemo nostrum idem est in senectute qui fuit iuvenes. (Seneca, Ep., 6; 58, 22)

= No-one us (subject) the same is in old age who was young.

Which means “None of us is the same in old age as in youth.”

0.3 Ablative

Like the name itself, ablative is about one complement “a/ab,” or “from, by” in English, and the sentence with ablative looks like this in English: “The bodies are mutilated by the beasts.” And also, the name ablative itself is also from Latin word “ablatives,” or “to have been taken away” in English.

Let’s use the sentence above “The bodies are mutilated by the beasts” as an example here:

Ferae laniant corpora. (active)

= Beasts mutilate the bodies.

Corpora a feris laniantur. (passive)

= Bodies are mutilated by the beasts.

We can see how “a” is used here and how it turned “copora” from object (the beasts mutilate “the object”) into subject (“the subject” is something that got mutilated by the beast).

0.4 Glimpse of Declensions

Declensions are highly important in Latin since knowing these declensions, or word conjugations in Latin could help us tell the subject, the direct/indirect object, and any other parts of a sentence which may confuse those who just begin learning Latin. For example,

  1. Salutat servus dominam.
  2. Servus dominam salutat.

These two sentences means the same since “servus” which ends with -us indicates that it’s the subject of the sentence, while “dominam” which ends with -am indicates that it’s a direct object. So they both mean, “The slave greets the mistress.”

However, in this set, things are different:

  1. Salutat servum domina.
  2. Servum domina salutat.

These two sentences means “The mistress greets the slave.” Have you noticed the differences?– dominam (direct object, 1st declension) turned into domina (subject) and servus (subject) turned into servum (direct object, 2nd declension) , in these two sentences. So, unlike English where we have static positions for every word, we can change word order easily in Latin. It must be confusing at beginning, but after you adapt its own rules, it will surely become something really convenient.

As for the detailed rules of these declensions, we will talk about this in the later part. The aim of course 0 is to help you to have a outline of Latin laguage itself.
