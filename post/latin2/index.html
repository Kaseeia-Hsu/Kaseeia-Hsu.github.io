2.0 Cases

We’ve mentioned the concept of “cases” in Latin last time, and in this chapter, we’ll have a close look into all these different cases, and also, in this chapter, we’ll read some paragraphs in Latin together for the first time! So, without further ado, let’s get into the topic.

  • Nominative: Nominative is the case for subject.
    • Ego manduco carnem. -> “Ego” is the subject here.
    • Caro manducata est a me. -> “Caro” is the subject here.
    • We can also use the auxilary verb “esse(to be)” to construct a nominal predicate, such as “Italia paeninsula est. (Italy is a peninsula.)” or “Puella puchra est. (The girl is beautiful.)”
  • Genitive: Genitive is a case which is used to indicate possession. It’s usually used with nouns and pronouns. The concept is really simple; for example, in English, we use “‘s,” or “his/her…” to indicate possession.
    • Terra agricolae. (Land of the farmer / The farmer’s land.) The word “agricolae” here is under the conjugation of genitive, which means “farmer’s.”
  • Dative: Dative is the case for indirect subject.
    • Puella cara est mihi. (The girl is dear to me.) The word “mihi” here is indirect object.
  • Accusative: Accusative is the case for direct object.
    • Ego manduco carnem. (I eat meat.) The word “carnem” here is direct object.
    • Ad Roman eo. (I go to Rome.) The word “Roman” here is direct object, while “Ad” means “to,” and “eo” means “(I) go.”
    • In casam ambulo. (I stroll inside the house.) The word “casam” here is direct object. while “In” means “in,” and “ambulo” means “stroll.”
  • Vocative: vocative is the case to be put into use when someone is calling the other person.
    • Ave Caesar! (Hi, Caesar!)
  • Ablative: This is the most difficult case in Latin. It’s used to express meanings that other cases can’t express. It’s often use with prepositions (preposition + noun).
    • de (about): De re publica loquimar. (We talk about politics.)
    • cum (with): Cum amico loquor. (I’m talking with a friend.)
    • in (in): Cicero in foro stabat. (Cicero was in the forum.)
    • e/ex (leave from): Ex Roma exeo. (I leave from Rome.)
    • a/ab (from): Ab urbe condita. (From the foundation of Rome.)

2.1 Declensions

2.1.1 First Declension

Words with first declension often end with -a and most of them are feminine. For example, rosa(rose).

Singular Plural
Nominative Ros-a Ros-ae
Genitive Ros-ae Ros-arum
Dative Ros-ae Ros-is
Accusative Ros-am Ros-as
Vocative Ros-a Ros-ae
Ablative Ros-a Ros-is

Exercise 2.1.1.1

Italia est magna paeninsula, terra fecunda. Paeninsula abundat pomis. Italia agricolarum et nautarum patria est. Aurae semper benignae sunt et aqua limpida est. Non sunt leaenae et pantherae. Feminae formosae sunt.

Word by word analysis: Italy is big peninsula, land fertile (n+adj.). Peninsula abound fruits (abundat + abl., so pomis is plural+abl. form of “pomum,” “fruits”) Italy of farmers (agricola -> pl.+gen. -> agricolarum | 1st declension) and of sailors nation is. (Italy is a nation of farmers and sailors.) Winds (1st. declension, pl.) always kind (benignae also follows the declension with aurae) are and water clear (limpida, orginally limpidus, but aqua is feminine noun, so limpidus follow the declension of aqua, and became limpida) is. No are (Non sunt = There are no) lionesses and panthers (leaena et panthera turned into nominative pl. form). Women beautiful are.

Exercise 2.1.1.2

Agricolae terram arant, glebas irrigant, aristas excitant, plantas currant, in silvis feras captant.

Farmers land (terra->terr-am | accusative sing.), soils (gleba->gleb-as | acc. pl.) irrigate, crops raise, sprouts take care, in forest (silva->silvis | abl. pl., since when we use in+location without any motion, we use ablative.) beasts capture.

Exercise 2.1.1.3

Fortuna apud antiquos dea erat, dea magna et valida. Summa cum inconstantia divitas et inopiam, gloriam et ignominiam, bona et mala distribuebat: nam dea caeca erat.

Fortuna (Goddess of Fortune) among ancient-time goddess was, goddess great and powerful (Magnus->magna, valedus->valid | Following dea’s gender). Total with fickleness riches and scarcity, glory and humiliation, good and evil distribute__(3rd person conjugation)__: because/since goddess blind was.
